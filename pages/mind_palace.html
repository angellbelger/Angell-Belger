Minutes earlier, the Greek poet Simonides of Ceos had risen to deliver an ode in honor of Scopas, a noble Thessalian. As Simonides sat back down, a messenger tapped him on the shoulder. Two young horsemen awaited outside, eager to share some news. He rose again and walked out the door. At the exact moment he crossed the threshold, the roof of the banquet hall collapsed, raising a cloud of dust and marble shards. He now stood amidst a landscape of ruins and buried bodies. The air, once filled with raucous laughter, turned misty and silent.

Rescue teams feverishly began excavating the collapsed building. The bodies they pulled from the wreckage were mutilated beyond recognition. No one could say for certain who had been inside. One tragedy compounded another.

Then, something remarkable happened that would forever change how people thought about their memories. Simonides sealed his senses to the chaos around him and reversed time in his mind. Marble stacks returned to pillars, scattered frieze fragments regrouped in the air. The debris on the ground transformed into bowls. Wooden splinters projecting over the ruins once again became a table. Simonides glimpsed each banquet guest in their seat, oblivious to the impending disaster.

He saw Scopas laughing at the head of the table, a fellow poet sitting in front of him cleaning the remnants of his meal with a piece of bread, a noble smiling. He turned to the window and saw the messengers approaching, as if bearing important news.

Simonides opened his eyes. He took each hysterical relative by the hand and, carefully stepping on the debris, guided them, one by one, to the points in the wreckage where their loved ones had been seated. In that moment, according to legend, the art of memory was born.
